Featured Projects

Reforestation

Best for restoring forests and dedicated carbon removal.

Reforestation projects plant thousands of trees, oftentimes reverting degraded land cleared for cattle or agriculture back to forest. Of the three forest project types, they are typically the best for quickly drawing CO2 out of the atmosphere, because young trees draw down more carbon from the atmosphere than fully grown trees.Read more

Forest conservation

Protect old growth forests from deforestation.

Forest Conservation (Avoided Deforestation) Best for protecting old-growth forest and wildlife habitat in areas a risk of deforestation. Forest conservation (also called avoided deforestation in the carbon world) aims to protect the enormous carbon stored in existing old-growth forests. Forest conservation projects protect habitat for a wide range of flora and fauna, including these Hoatzins found in the Madre de Dios project.Read more

Improved forest management

Best for managing existing forests to produce forest products and maintain natural ecosystems.

The McCloud River Project combines harvesting with long term land management also includes wildfire management. Improved Forest Management (IFM) projects are critical carbon projects that draw down carbon, maintain species habitat, and allow the sustainable harvest of timber all at the same time. Read more

Reforestation: Ecosystem Restoration

Pure ecosystem restoration projects work to regrow land with native species in hopes of restoring ecosystems and preserving them in the long run. Fazendo Sao Nicolao in Mato Grosso, Brazil is an example of a project funded for the purpose of capturing carbon and restoring biodiversity. Fazendo Sao Nicolao has planted 26 native species over almost 2,000 hectares since 1999 on land previously deforested for cattle grazing.

Reforestation: Tree Plantation

Other reforestation projects combine tree planting with economic timber models where projects grow trees that are scheduled for harvest down the line. Even though these projects harvest timber they plant, they earn carbon credits because they commit to maintaining an average amount of carbon stock over the course of the project — and they should show that the project would not have occurred without carbon funding.
